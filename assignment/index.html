The World Happiness Report is a survey that ranks 156 countries based on how happy the residents perceive themselves to be. The results are based on six main factors: GDP, life expectancy, generosity, social support, freedom and corruption. This visualisation and data is based on the World Happiness Report 2020, its eighth edition overall.

World Happiness vs Life Expectancy

The average Life Expectancy of a country is one of the key factors that the authors use in determining a country's Happiness Score. In this chart, countries are sorted by region and are plotted with its happiness score and average life expectancy. Therefore, it is expected to see a positive relationship between the two variables. There are, however, some outliers. For example, Hong Kong has one of the highest life expectancies but a middling happiness score of only 5.51. Other countries of note are also labelled.

Hover over a country's dot to see its statistics! You can also filter for a specific region by clicking it in the legend section or selecting it in the dropdown menu. Additionally, use the scroll wheel to zoom in and out of the visualisation.

Next, we sort the countries by region and add them to a boxplot. Here, we can see the quartiles as well as the minimum and maximum score for each region.

North America and ANZ and Western Europe (more developed countries) have a high score overall. Latin America has the third highest median score, but is weighed down by its lowest scoring country, Haiti.

Middle East & North Africa, Sub-Saharan Africa, and South Asia all also have a large range, likely due to outliers.

Life Expectancy is also sorted by region so we can see their quartiles, minimum and maximum scores.

North America and ANZ, all of Europe and East Asia have the highest life expectancy. Interestingly, the former two both have small ranges meaning that most countries in that region have around the same score.

Countries in Africa and South Asia have the lowest life expectancy globally, likely due to poor conditions and healthcare. Again, we can see that Latin America has a large range, suggesting a wide disparity between countries in that region, namely Haiti and Costa Rica.

Unemployment Rate and GDP per Capita

GDP per Capita is another key factor in determining a country's happiness score. It is calculated in PPP (purchasing power parity) dollars. While it is not a considered a factor, Unemployment Rate (percentage of labour force unemployed) is also an key figure that may affect people's happiness overall. Here, we compare these two statistics to see if there is any correlation.

Unemployment Rate is sorted from lowest to highest, while GDP per Capita is sorted from highest to lowest. Hovering over a bar will also show the country's ranking amongst the data. Clicking on a bar will allow you to easily compare the values and rankings of the two variables for the same country. For example, Qatar has the lowest unemployment rate and the second-highest GDP per capita!
